**Pushback (2): some specific numerical thresholds are tighter than the data can support.** Gemini has specified |m_B1| ≤ 0.03 NCME per D_M and baseline mean NCME ≥ 0.85. These are not unreasonable as point predictions but they're committed without reference to actual measurement noise in published activation-patching work. The Wang et al. Better practice: pilot a known ToM-relevant circuit on the chosen model first, measure actual NCME variance on matched-control vs treatment pairs, and set the B1 invariance threshold at N times the empirical noise standard deviation (probably N = 2 or 3). The framework Gemini has built is right; the specific numerical commitments should be marked as provisional pending pilot calibration.

**Pushback (3): the asymptote lim(X→10) NCME ≤ 0.15 for B2 is potentially overfitted.** B2 is interpolation; an interpolator's behaviour at very high distance from training is not necessarily an asymptote toward zero, it could plateau at the model's prior over the correct token. The threshold may be tighter than B2 actually predicts. Better to leave the asymptote as ≤ 0.40 (well below B1's predicted 0.85+) and rely on the slope and breakpoint criteria to discriminate, rather than over-specifying a tail value that's mechanism-uncertain.

This is not just a "calibrate against pilot noise floor" issue; it's a potential confound that biases the [recon: result toward B2. Better practice: validate the novel condition] without quantisation by comparing to a smaller un-quantised model (Pythia-2.8B class) where the comparison is cleaner; pre-specify in the prereg that quantisation-induced variance is a known confound and that conclusions are robust only if the qualitative pattern holds across precision levels. **Pushback (5): Gemini's open gap (c) on ACDC's τ is underspecified.** Setting tau too loose admits polysemantic-neuron noise, as Gemini notes. But setting it too tight forces ACDC to recover only the most attribution-heavy edges and may miss components that contribute to the circuit but aren't first-order important. The protocol needs both an explicit τ value and a sensitivity analysis: report whether the circuit identified is qualitatively stable across at least three τ values spanning an order of magnitude. If the identified circuit changes character across τ, the circuit-identification stage is too noisy for the subsequent NCME analysis to mean what it claims. Add this as a required robustness check rather than an open gap.
